Dwapar Yug, Dronacharya and the elephant

Without going into details, let me try and drive the point.

It is the time of the Kurukshetra War. After the death of Bheeshmacharya, Dronacharya becomes the commander-in-chief of the Kaurava army. Krishna knew that Dronacharya was formidable and with him in charge, it was impossible to defeat the Kauravas.

The only way to remove Dronacharya was to convince him that his son, Ashwathama, was dead. With his son gone, Dronacharya would give up and he could only then be killed. Krishna, the greatest trickster, devises a plan.

This, for me, is a classic case of the framing effect. Dronacharya placed enormous trust in Yudhisthir and believed that Yudhisthir would never make a false statement and when Yudhisthir called out that Ashwathama was dead, he instantaneously believed it. That is how the framing effect works. It is not the information, but the way it is presented that does the trick. The trick here is to ‚Äòframe‚Äô someone.

Framing Effect

We often draw different conclusions from the same information depending on how it is presented.

The framing effect perhaps is one of the most common tricks used by marketers, politicians, news agencies, influencers and not to forget ‚Äî magicians. As the name itself says, it ‚Äòframes‚Äô the audience into believing what they see (or hear as it was in Dronacharya‚Äôs case), depending on how the information is presented. And based on the way information is presented, we draw different conclusions from it ‚Äî to support a party or not, to buy a shoe or not, to trust a person or not ‚Äî we do it almost all the time. You could draw a completely wrong conclusion and only realise it much later or not realise it at all! You could have drawn an entirely different conclusion had the information been presented in any different way.

What am I trying to say, anyway?

Anyway, coming to the point. The information you consume, from the people you know, from advertisements, from strangers on social media, from newspapers or any other media ‚Äî they all bear a very high power to influence us. It can sway us away from one view to another extreme view, merely by the way information is presented.

People tend to present information that suits their narrative, in a way that doesn‚Äôt harm them but helps them. It is natural. It stems from the need for self-protection and preservation. People don‚Äôt give away their shortcomings as easily as they glorify their virtues.

In fact, the onus of identifying the shortcomings (or all the facts) is on the other person. This is prevalent in almost everything you do ‚Äî purchasing a car, downloading an App, ordering food, looking for a match on matrimony, voting in an election, selecting an insurance plan ‚Äî the list is endless.

In fact, ‚ÄòCaveat Emptor‚Äô, a Latin phrase, which can be loosely translated into English as ‚Äòlet the buyers beware‚Äô is a law in commercial transactions. By law, the onus is on you while making a purchase.

And this rule, if not law, can be extended beyond commercial transactions. While consuming information, the onus is on the receiver to get the complete information. There always will be grey areas in the information presented and you need to be careful while you draw conclusions from the information presented as it is.

This happens all the time ‚Äî from making new friends to planning a trip, from finding a partner to buying a new house, from choosing a college to starting a company. But if not paid enough attention, this could take you for a ride.
